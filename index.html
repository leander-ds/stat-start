How is this worked out?

These figures summarise one set of values: where the centre lies and how spread out the values are. The mean is the average and the median is the middle value; when they pull apart, the data are skewed. The standard deviation is the typical distance of a value from the mean.

mean = Σx / n    SD = √(Σ(x − mean)² / (n − 1))

How is this worked out?
A correlation measures whether two variables move together, and how closely. Pearson’s r runs from −1 to +1: the sign gives the direction and the size gives the strength. It is found by putting both variables on a common scale and averaging how their pairs match up.
r = covariance(x, y) / (SD of x · SD of y)

Two worlds. If nothing is going on (H₀) the test statistic lands in the left curve; if your effect is real (H₁) it lands in the right. The cutoff turns each into a decision. 

          

          

        

        

          
What am I looking at?

          
Every study is a bet made on one sample. The left curve is the world where the groups are truly identical (H₀); the right curve is the world where the effect is real (H₁). You pick a cutoff, past it you declare a finding. The red sliver beyond the cutoff under H₀ is a false alarm (α, Type I error); the amber area under H₁ that falls short of the cutoff is a miss (β, Type II error); the green area is power, your chance of catching a real effect. Move the sliders and watch the green grow.

How is this worked out?
Power is the chance a test comes out significant when a real effect is present. It rises with three things: a larger true effect, a larger sample, and a less strict significance threshold. The plot shows how changing each one moves power up or down.

How is this worked out?
The central limit theorem explains why so many tests assume a normal distribution. If you take many samples and record each sample mean, those means form a bell shape, even when the raw values do not. The larger each sample, the tighter and more normal that bell becomes.

How is this worked out?
A chi-square test checks whether two categorical variables are related. For each cell it compares the count you observed with the count expected if the variables were unrelated, worked out from the row and column totals. The further the observed counts sit from the expected ones, the larger the statistic.
expected = (row total × column total) / grand total    χ² = Σ (O − E)² / E
